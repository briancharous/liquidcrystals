EM Waves

Light, radio waves, gamma rays, etc. are all types of electromagnetic radiation. Basically, that means that light is carried by an alternating electric and magnetic field. In a vacuum, EM waves propagate at the speed of light, 2.998x10^8 m/s.

This image shows a representation of a propagating electromagnetic wave. Notice that the electric field component (labeled E) is perpendicular to the magnetic field component (labeled B).

Polarization

Polarization of an electromagnetic wave describes the plane of the electric field vector. In linear polarization, the electric field vector is confined to one plane. The image in the previous section shows a linearly polarized EM wave. There is also circular polarization, in which the direction of the electric field vector changes, but the magnitude does not; elliptical polarization is when the tip of the electric field vector traces out an ellipse as it propagates. More often than not, when the term "polarized" is used, we are referring to linearly polarized light, but not necessarily.

All electromagnetic waves are polarized. However, that doesn't mean that a collection of waves all have the same polarization. Light given off by most sources is unpolarized. This just means that all the light waves coming out of the source have different polarizations. A light source that gives off polarized light is one that emits light whose waves all have the same polarization. A common example of a polarized emitter is a laser.

Polarizers

A polarizer is simply a device that takes unpolarized light or light of a different polarization and changes it into a specific polarization.

This image shows a a linear polarizer. As unpolarized light comes in from the left, the linear polarizer only lets light with a vertical electric field component through. Light that is polarized in the direction of the polarizer gets completely through. Light with some other polarization only gets partially through.

A useful property of linear polarizers is that they completely block all light with linear polarization rotated by 90 degrees from the direction of their polarization. Thus, if we place two linear polarizers on top of each other, and rotate one by 90 degrees with respect to the other one, all light will be blocked.
