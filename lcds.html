How a pixel works

To make a subpixel for an LCD, nematic liquid crystals are placed in between two polarizers rotated at 90 degrees from each other. Transparent electrodes are also placed around the liquid crystals. The liquid crystals are twisted in between the polarizers. As light passes through the liquid crystal, the plane of polarization follows the liquid crystals twist. Thus, when the light reaches the opposite polarizer, it will have the same polarization as the top polarizer and the pixel will appear white. As an electric field is applied, the liquid crystals rotate in the direction of the field. Eventually, the light will only see one index of refraction and the polarization will not change. All the light will be blocked by the second polarizer and the pixel will appear black.

Creating a full-color LCD

To create a full color LCD, each pixel is really 3 pixels. Each sub-pixel is covered by a filter that only lets red, green, or blue light through. By varying the intensity of the red, green, and blue sub-pixel a full array of colors can be created. Putting millions of these 3 sub-pixel arrangements together creates a full-color screen.
